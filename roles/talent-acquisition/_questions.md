### 1.2 Core Technical Questions

Standard questions for this role, not tailored to any specific candidate.

### Q1: Walk me through your typical end-to-end recruitment process — from the moment you receive a job brief to the point an offer is extended.

**What a good answer looks like:**
- Can articulate each stage of the funnel: intake meeting with hiring manager, sourcing strategy, screening criteria, interview coordination, debrief, offer
- Mentions how they calibrate the job brief if requirements are unclear or unrealistic
- Shows awareness of where drop-off happens and how they manage pipeline health

**Red flags:**
- Describes only one or two stages (e.g., just posting jobs and scheduling calls) with no sense of the full funnel
- No mention of partnering with hiring managers or iterating on the brief
- Treats offer extension as the finish line with no thought for candidate experience or fallback

---

### Q2: How do you source passive candidates — people who aren't actively looking? Walk me through a specific channel or tactic you've used and how you know it worked.

**What a good answer looks like:**
- Names specific channels beyond LinkedIn (professional communities, niche job boards, referral programs, alumni networks, etc.)
- Can describe the outreach message or hook they use and how they tailor it per role
- Mentions a concrete outcome: response rates, hires made, pipeline built

**Red flags:**
- Sourcing strategy is limited to job posting and waiting
- Cannot describe how they personalize outreach or why a candidate would respond
- No sense of measurement — can't say whether the tactic actually produced candidates

---

### Q3: How do you screen resumes and run initial screening calls? What signals are you looking for beyond whether someone meets the stated requirements?

**What a good answer looks like:**
- Has a structured approach to resume review — knows what to look for and what to deprioritize
- Uses the screening call to assess motivation, culture fit, and communication — not just checking boxes
- Can articulate how they calibrate their screening criteria against the hiring manager's actual priorities (not just the written JD)

**Red flags:**
- Screening is described as purely checklist-based against the JD
- No mention of how they assess soft fit or motivation
- Cannot give an example of passing or declining a candidate based on a nuanced signal

---

### Q4: Describe your experience with Applicant Tracking Systems. Which ones have you used, and what data or reports do you actually look at to manage your pipeline?

**What a good answer looks like:**
- Names specific ATS tools used (e.g., Greenhouse, Lever, Workday, or local equivalents)
- Uses the ATS actively — not just as a calendar but to track pipeline velocity, stage conversion, time-to-fill
- Mentions at least one instance where data from the ATS changed how they approached a role or process

**Red flags:**
- ATS use is limited to scheduling or storing resumes
- Cannot name meaningful metrics they track (time-to-hire, offer acceptance rate, source of hire)
- Describes the ATS as a burden rather than a tool they actively manage

---

### Q5: Tell me about a time a role you were recruiting for was very hard to fill — either because the requirements were narrow, the market was thin, or the timeline was aggressive. What did you do?

**What a good answer looks like:**
- Describes a specific difficult role, not a generic approach
- Proactively expanded sourcing strategy, pushed back on requirements, or negotiated the timeline with the hiring manager
- Honest about what worked and what didn't — not a clean-win story

**Red flags:**
- "Hard" role turns out to be standard difficulty in hindsight
- Response is purely reactive — waited for more applicants or extended the deadline without trying something different
- No mention of looping in the hiring manager or adjusting expectations

---

### Q6: How do you manage multiple open roles at the same time while still hitting your pipeline targets? What does your prioritization approach look like?

**What a good answer looks like:**
- Has a concrete system: priority tiers by urgency/seniority, time-blocking, pipeline health checks
- Can describe what signals tell them a role needs more attention versus is on track
- Mentions how they communicate progress (or blockers) to hiring managers proactively

**Red flags:**
- Prioritization is reactive — responds to whoever is loudest
- No structure described — "just stays on top of it"
- Cannot describe how they track pipeline health across multiple roles simultaneously

## 2. Behavioral Interview

Not a technical assessment — this section probes a role-specific selection of traits drawn from `roles/_behavioral_question_bank.md` (Ownership, Grit, Candidate Empathy, Conflict / Difficult Conversations, Receiving Feedback, Integrity). Evidence for any trait can surface in response to any question below (or even during the Technical Interview) — the rubric is scored from the totality of the transcript, not one-to-one against the question that "belongs" to it.

### 2.1 Behavioral Rubric

| Grit | [ ] Green  [ ] Amber  [ ] Red | |
| Candidate Empathy | [ ] Green  [ ] Amber  [ ] Red | |
| Conflict / Difficult Conversations | [ ] Green  [ ] Amber  [ ] Red | |
| Receiving Feedback | [ ] Green  [ ] Amber  [ ] Red | |
| Integrity | [ ] Green  [ ] Amber  [ ] Red | |

**Anchors — copied verbatim from `roles/_behavioral_question_bank.md` for each selected trait, do not redesign:**

**Ownership**
- Green — Uses "I" for failure points, "we" for wins. Proactively expanded scope without being asked. Follows through to resolution, not just identification.
- Amber — Claims some ownership but softens it — acknowledges a failure existed but frames their role ambiguously, or expanded scope once but describes it as an exceptional one-off rather than a pattern.
- Red — Failure is always someone else's fault or "the process." Identifies problems but has no story of actually fixing one outside their lane.

**Grit**
- Green — Honest about the cost (burnout, strained relationships, doubt) rather than a triumphalist story. Can articulate *why* they persisted, not just that they did.
- Amber — Describes a genuinely hard stretch but glosses over the cost or the reason for persisting, defaulting to "I just push through" without deeper reflection.
- Red — Glorifies overwork with no reflection on sustainability. No moment of real doubt in the story — suspiciously clean narrative.

**Candidate Empathy**
- Green — Specific attention to the other person's experience, not just the mechanics of delivering news. Evidence of actually changing behavior based on someone else's feedback/pain, not just noting it.
- Amber — Describes delivering difficult news or noticing others' experience, but the response was situational rather than leading to any lasting change in approach.
- Red — Empathy described only in the abstract ("I always try to be kind"). No concrete instance of adjusting a decision because of someone else's experience.

**Conflict / Difficult Conversations**
- Green — Addresses the conflict directly rather than avoiding or escalating past it. Reflects on the relationship post-conflict, not just the immediate resolution.
- Amber — Describes a real tense disagreement but the resolution is vague, or reflection stops at "it worked out" without detail on how the relationship was maintained.
- Red — Conflict always resolved by them being "right" and the other person coming around. Avoidance dressed up as diplomacy ("I just let it go").

**Receiving Feedback**
- Green — Distinguishes between initial emotional reaction and eventual response — honest that it stung before it was useful. Can describe feedback they *didn't* fully agree with and how they handled that tension productively (not just capitulation).
- Amber — Describes real feedback and an eventual change, but skips the initial reaction/friction, or the disagreement was resolved by simply deferring rather than genuinely working through it.
- Red — Claims to have never received hard feedback, or that all feedback was immediately and gratefully accepted. No example of pushing back on feedback they thought was wrong.

**Integrity**
- Green — Chose transparency even when it was costly (career, relationship, timeline). Specific, not hypothetical.
- Amber — Describes a real ethical or professional tension but the resolution is unclear or was deferred to someone else, or the "cost" of honesty turns out to have been minimal.
- Red — Answer is entirely hypothetical ("I would..." rather than "I did..."). Frames dishonesty-adjacent behavior as clever or resourceful.

### 2.2 Behavioral Questions (STAR)

All questions must be phrased to elicit a STAR response (Situation → Task → Action → Result).

### B1: Tell me about a project or role where something fell short — maybe a hiring target you missed, a candidate you lost, or a process that failed. What was your role in that, and what did you do about it?

**Primary trait probed:** Ownership

**What to listen for:**
- Uses "I" for failure points and owns the gap rather than attributing it to circumstances or other teams
- Describes a concrete action taken to fix or prevent recurrence — not just identification of the problem
- Follows through to resolution; didn't hand it off and move on

---

### B2: Tell me about the hardest sustained period of work you've had in recruitment — not a single crunch day, but a stretch of weeks or months where the volume, pressure, or stakes were high. What kept you going?

**Primary trait probed:** Grit

**What to listen for:**
- Honest about the cost — fatigue, doubt, trade-offs made — rather than a clean triumph story
- Can articulate why they persisted (not just "I push through"), connecting it to something that mattered to them
- Shows some reflection on sustainability, not just glorifying the grind

---

### B3: Tell me about a time you had to give a candidate difficult news — a rejection, a withdrawn offer, or a sudden change in the role. How did you approach that conversation?

**Primary trait probed:** Candidate Empathy

**What to listen for:**
- Specific attention to the candidate's experience, not just the mechanics of delivering the news
- Evidence they thought about how it would land, not just what they needed to say
- Changed their approach for future situations based on how it went — not just a one-time adjustment

---

### B4: Describe a time you had a real disagreement with a hiring manager — about the candidate bar, the timeline, the process, or a specific hire. How did you handle it, and what happened to the relationship afterward?

**Primary trait probed:** Conflict / Difficult Conversations

**What to listen for:**
- Addresses the disagreement directly rather than going along or escalating past the hiring manager
- Can describe the specific point of tension and what they actually said
- Reflects on the relationship afterward — not just "we resolved it"

---

### B5: Tell me about the most critical piece of feedback you've received about your work as a recruiter. What was your first reaction, and what did you do with it?

**Primary trait probed:** Receiving Feedback

**What to listen for:**
- Honest that there was an initial reaction (defensive, stung, surprised) before they found the feedback useful
- Describes a concrete change in behavior that followed — not just "I took it on board"
- If they disagreed with the feedback, can explain how they handled that tension without just deferring

---

### B6: Have you ever been in a situation where you were under pressure to move a candidate forward — or screen one out — in a way that didn't sit right with you? What did you do?

**Primary trait probed:** Integrity

**What to listen for:**
- Describes a real situation, not a hypothetical — "I did" not "I would"
- Chose transparency or pushback even when it had a cost (friction with a hiring manager, missing a target, slowing the process)
- Does not frame working around the system as resourcefulness
